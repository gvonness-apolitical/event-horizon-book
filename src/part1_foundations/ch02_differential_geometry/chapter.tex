\chapter{Differential Geometry for General Relativity}
\label{ch:differential-geometry}

Special relativity gives us a geometry for flat spacetime, but the central
lesson of general relativity is that gravity \emph{is} geometry: freely falling
observers experience no gravitational force, and what we call gravity is the
curvature of spacetime itself.  To describe this curvature --- and to trace
photons through it --- we need a calculus that works on spaces where the metric
varies from point to point, where partial derivatives no longer transform as
tensors, and where the notion of a ``straight line'' must be replaced by
something more subtle.

The chapter builds this calculus in five steps.  We begin with the bare notion
of a smooth manifold --- a space that looks locally like $\mathbb{R}^n$ but may
curve globally --- and equip it with a metric tensor that defines distances,
angles, and the causal structure inherited from \cref{ch:special-relativity}.
The metric determines a unique derivative operator, the covariant derivative,
whose connection coefficients appear explicitly in the codebase's metric
modules.  With this derivative in hand, we define geodesics and recast them in
the Hamiltonian form that the ray tracer integrates at every step.  The chapter
culminates with the Riemann curvature tensor, which encodes how the geometry
deviates from flatness and feeds directly into the Einstein field equations of
\cref{ch:general-relativity}.

The two-sphere $S^2$ serves as a running worked example: coordinates, metric,
Christoffel symbols, geodesics, and curvature are all computed explicitly for
this familiar geometry before the same tools are applied to four-dimensional
spacetimes.  Readers comfortable with differential geometry but unfamiliar with
our conventions may wish to start with \cref{sec:dg-metric} for notation and
\cref{sec:dg-geodesic} for the Hamiltonian formulation that drives the
integrator.
