\chapter{Finite Difference Methods}
\label{ch:finite-differences}

The metrics and geodesics of Parts~I and~II are exact: analytic expressions
evaluated at whatever spacetime point the integrator needs.  But the evolution
equations of numerical relativity --- the BSSN system of
\cref{ch:bssn-ccz4}, the gauge conditions that determine how coordinates
evolve, the constraint equations that monitor solution quality --- are partial
differential equations on a three-dimensional grid.  Grid data is discrete;
the equations demand spatial derivatives.  Finite differences supply them,
approximating each derivative as a weighted sum of neighbouring field values
with an error that shrinks systematically as the grid is refined.

The construction is remarkably clean.  Taylor-expanding a grid function about
a point and solving for derivative approximations of a chosen order yields a
\emph{stencil}: a fixed pattern of integer offsets and rational coefficients
that converts discrete data into a derivative estimate.  The codebase uses
fourth-order centred stencils for all spatial derivative operators: five-point
stencils for first and second derivatives, a separable two-pass pattern for
mixed partials that replaces a scattered twenty-five-point access with two
reuses of the one-dimensional stencil, and a
seven-point Kreiss--Oliger dissipation operator that selectively damps
grid-scale noise.  Fourth-order accuracy means that doubling the grid
resolution reduces the truncation error by a factor of sixteen --- a concrete,
testable prediction that the convergence-testing framework verifies for every
numerical scheme in the book.

The chapter derives the stencil coefficients from Taylor expansion
(\cref{sec:fd-stencils}), writes out the explicit second- and fourth-order
formulae for first and second derivatives (\cref{sec:fd-orders}), formalises
the truncation error that controls the relationship between resolution and
accuracy (\cref{sec:fd-truncation}), introduces Kreiss--Oliger dissipation as
the mechanism that keeps long-running evolutions stable without degrading
convergence order (\cref{sec:fd-dissipation}), and establishes the
verification methodology --- manufactured solutions and self-convergence
tests --- used throughout the numerical-methods and numerical-relativity
chapters (\cref{sec:fd-convergence}).
